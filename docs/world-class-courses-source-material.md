## Session 1: Power Your Students to Action

**Subtitle**: The Fundamental Framework Behind Engaging Online Courses

### The Market Shift

The opening question — "How has the buying of online courses changed over the past few years?" — frames the entire series. The implicit answer: buyers have become more sophisticated. They've been burned by content-dump courses. They now evaluate courses on outcomes, not volume.

### The 4 Keys to Powerful Online Courses

Abe's core framework. These four keys structure the entire 4-session curriculum:

1. **Outcome-Focused Design** (Session 1-2) — Start with what the student will achieve, not what you'll teach
2. **Learner-Centric Structure** (Session 2-3) — Design around who you serve, not around your expertise
3. **Engaging Learning Experience** (Session 3) — Structure, sequencing, balanced modules
4. **Community & Accountability** (Session 4) — Support systems that drive completion

### The 2 Traps of Ineffective Learning Design

This is one of the most distinctive and actionable frameworks in the series:

**Trap 1: The Instructionism Trap**
- The default mode for most course creators: "telling" instead of engaging
- Manifests as: talking-head videos, lecture-style delivery, passive consumption
- Root cause: **The Curse of Knowledge** — once you know something, it's hard to remember what it's like not to know it. Experts organize information in ways that make sense *to experts*, not to beginners.
- The fix: Design for *doing*, not for *knowing*. Every piece of content should connect to an action the student takes.

**Trap 2: The Aboutness Trap**
- Creating a course "about" a topic vs. solving a problem or helping improve performance, results, or experiences
- Conceiving the course as being "about" a topic rather than helping people move forward
- Symptoms:
  - **Incoherent organization & sequencing** — modules organized by topic taxonomy (how the expert thinks about the field) rather than by learning progression (how a student needs to encounter ideas)
  - **"Overdosing" / "Inappropriate dosing"** (term credited to Jen Louden) — overloaded content and activities. Too much, too fast, without regard for cognitive load or what students actually need at each stage. The opposite of appropriate dosing.
- The fix: Organize around the student's journey, not the subject's structure. "What does the student need *next* to take their next step?" — not "What's the next topic in my outline?"

### Outcome-Focused Design

The bridge from "traps" to "solutions":
- Start with the transformation, not the content
- Work backward from the desired outcome to determine what's actually needed
- Strip away everything that doesn't serve the outcome

### Dual Minimalism (from MVO Discussion)

The solution to both traps is **outcome-focused design with dual minimalism**:

1. **Focused, well-scoped outcomes that deliver value** — don't try to teach everything; define the specific result
2. **Minimal content to scaffold progress toward those outcomes** — only include what's needed to get there

This is the antidote to both the Aboutness Trap (broad topic → narrow outcome) and overdosing (maximum content → minimum viable content).

The starting question: "Based on everything I've learned from my research and talking to customers, **what do people really want from my course?**"

In Mirasee's LMC program, one technique for this is **"Magic Wanding"** — imagining the ideal outcome with no constraints. But it's equally important to reflect on what people have been telling you in research calls. What is the solution they need, or the better experience they seek?

Then the scoping question: **"What can you realistically help them achieve in the scope of your pilot?"**

#### The 4 Scoping Questions

These questions help refine a course down to its minimum viable outcome:

1. **"How long will my pilot have to be to deliver on this promise?"** There are exceptions, but in most cases pilots shouldn't be more than 4-6 weeks. If you need much longer, scale back what you're delivering.

2. **"If someone started my pilot but quit after a month, would they see any benefits?"** If so, what would those be? How could you cram the most possible benefits into those first four weeks? Can you make those the *only* benefits offered in the pilot?

3. **"If whatever you're doing is yielding results, what would be the first big milestone they'd hit that gives them a real visceral sense that this is working?"** If that milestone is compelling enough, can you make it the entire promise of your pilot?

4. **"If you were on a two-hour plane ride with your ideal customer, what could you teach them in those two hours that would leave them saying, 'Wow, that really helped me'?"** Can you limit the scope of your pilot to whatever you'd teach in those two hours?

These questions progressively tighten the scope: 4-6 weeks → first month → first milestone → two hours. Each one forces the course creator to identify what's truly essential vs. what's nice-to-have. The plane ride question is especially powerful — it strips away everything except the highest-impact content.

### Design The Learning Experience

Preview of Sessions 3-4: once you know the outcome, design the experience that gets students there. This is where structure, sequencing, content, delivery, community, and accountability all come in.

## Session 2: If You Don't Know Where You're Going…

**Subtitle**: Designing Learner-Centric Courses

### Understand Your Learner's Needs & Context

The foundational exercise for the entire framework. Before designing anything, deeply understand:
- Who you serve
- What they already know
- What they struggle with
- What context they're operating in (time, resources, motivation, constraints)

### The Learner Persona

Not a marketing persona — a *learning* persona. Key distinction: marketing personas focus on buying motivations; learner personas focus on:
- **Motivation**: Why are they taking this course? What's driving them?
- **Problems**: What specific problems are they trying to solve?
- **Questions**: What do they need to understand?
- **Needs**: What support do they require to succeed?

This is a richer model than typical "avatar" exercises in the course creation space, because it centers the *learning journey* rather than the *purchase journey*.

### Envision Success: Know – Feel – Do

A framework credited to Yale University. For each learner, define three dimensions of success:

- **Know**: What will they understand? What knowledge will they have?
- **Feel**: What emotional state will they achieve? (Confidence, clarity, relief, motivation)
- **Do**: What will they be able to *do* that they couldn't before?

Most course creators focus only on "Know." The "Feel" and "Do" dimensions are where real transformation happens — and where premium pricing is justified.

### The Dream Exercise

A powerful visualization exercise:

> "Imagine that you have a group of dream students. They are engaged, they are perfectly behaved, and they have perfect memories. Fill in this sentence: **3-5 years from now, my students still know... Or they still are able to do... Or they still find value in...**"

This exercise shifts the designer's thinking from "what content do I need to deliver?" to "what lasting change am I creating?" The 3-5 year timeframe is deliberate — it forces you to identify durable outcomes, not transient information.

### Define Measurable Criteria

"How will we know whether our students have achieved the outcomes... or not?"

This is where Abe's framework becomes rigorous. Measurable criteria turn aspirational outcomes into verifiable results.

**Examples from the workshop:**

| Course Type | Measurable Criteria |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Stress reduction | Self-assessment score improves within 8 weeks |
| Novel writing | At least 5,000 words written + outline for the rest |
| Facebook ads | 10% improvement in leads from ads |

Key insight: The criteria don't have to be numeric — they need to be *observable*. "Students will feel more confident" is not measurable. "Students will pitch their services to 3 new prospects" is.

### Ideation Circles

The workshop's breakout format. Round-robin small groups where each participant:
1. Introduces themselves and their work
2. Presents their draft outcomes/criteria
3. Gets feedback and ideas from the group

Abe's explicit note: "Don't expect to come to exact clarity in this discussion. The goal is to **broaden your thinking**, and then apply the ideas later." This is a learning design move in itself — he's modeling the kind of peer-learning and ideation process he's teaching.

## Session 3: The Master Plan

**Subtitle**: Learner-Centric Course Structure & Content

### Design The Learning Experience — Three Pillars

**1. Ease of Access**
- Reduce friction to getting started and moving through the course
- If the platform, navigation, or logistics are confusing, learning suffers before it begins
- This connects directly to Abe's HCI research on information architecture and system usability

**2. Structure and Sequencing**
- How content is organized and ordered
- Must follow the learner's progression, not the expert's taxonomy
- Directly addresses the "Aboutness Trap" — incoherent organization comes from organizing by topic rather than by learning journey

**3. Content & Delivery**
- What you actually teach and how you deliver it
- Must be balanced (not all lecture, not all exercises)
- Must match the learner's needs at each stage

### The Iterative Piloting Process

A key practical insight:

> "You don't feel comfortable teaching the course until the 3rd time you run it..."

This is permission-giving and strategic. It means:
- Your first version will be rough. That's expected.
- Plan to revise based on real student experience
- Don't over-invest in production quality before you've validated the learning design
- Pilot → refine → pilot → refine → then polish

### Map the Path

Creating a first-pass course outline as a learning path:
- Start with the outcome (from Session 2)
- Work backward: what's the last thing they need to learn before achieving the outcome? What comes before that?
- Each step should be a meaningful milestone, not just a content section

### Balanced Learning Modules

Each module should balance multiple types of learning activity — not just content delivery. This connects to:
- **Active learning** research (doing, not just watching/reading)
- **Varied practice** (multiple modalities strengthen retention)
- **Cognitive load management** (alternating between input and application prevents overload)

The "Remember the 2 traps!" callback is deliberate — it's a structural reminder that the traps apply at every level of design, from overall course structure down to individual modules.

## Session 4: Staying on Track

**Subtitle**: The Power of Support & Accountability

### Community — Principles > Tools

A critical framing: the *principle* of community matters more than the *tool* you use. Facebook groups, Slack, in-platform discussion — the tool is secondary. The design principle is primary.

### Three Approaches to Community

**Approach #1: Social Presence**

Design principle: "Increase feeling of belonging and social motivation by making other participants' progress visible."

Examples:
- Visual indicators of other people in the course
- "X people have completed this..." counters
- Activity feeds showing what others are doing
- "High fives" and acknowledgment features

This is drawn from social learning theory — people are motivated by seeing others engaged in the same activity. It reduces the isolation of self-paced online learning.

**Approach #2: Discussion**

Design principle: "Increase feeling of belonging, and support peer learning, by making it easy to ask questions and share challenges."

Four implementation patterns compared:
1. **In-course discussions** (e.g., Ruzuku) — discussion integrated into the course flow at each step
2. **Standalone forums** — separate discussion space, topically organized
3. **Facebook groups** — external, familiar, but noisy and unstructured
4. **Slack channels** — real-time, threaded, but can be overwhelming

Key insight: In-course discussion (pattern 1) has the highest engagement because it's contextual — the discussion is *about* the specific content the student just encountered. Separate discussion spaces (patterns 2-4) require the student to context-switch, which reduces participation.

**Approach #3: Live Interaction**

Two design principles:
1. "Support regular progress through **scheduled sessions** (time blocking)" — live sessions create external structure that keeps students moving forward
2. "Increase self-efficacy by seeing **other people's questions and challenges**" — live interaction normalizes struggle and reduces imposter syndrome

### Accountability & Coaching

Framed with the MOOC completion statistic:

> "Among all MOOC participants, **3.13 percent** completed their courses in 2017-18" (Inside Higher Ed, 2019)

This is the problem that accountability solves. Without support structures, the default completion rate is catastrophically low.

**Peer Learning & Coaching models:**

1. **Accountability Partners & Groups**
   - Key design requirement: "Provide structure, agenda and materials"
   - "Help participants overcome the **logistics challenge**" — the biggest barrier to peer accountability isn't willingness, it's coordination. Provide a structure so partners know exactly what to do.

2. **Mastermind Groups**
   - Structured peer-to-peer learning in small groups
   - Requires clear process and rotating roles

3. **Peer Review Mechanisms**
   - Students review each other's work
   - Develops critical thinking skills while providing feedback
   - Scales instructor effort (you don't have to review everything yourself)

4. **Professional Facilitation**
   - Assignments & follow-up (proactive, not just reactive)
   - Monitor engagement & progress via course platform (data-driven intervention)
   - Schedule private coaching sessions (high-touch accountability)

## Cross-Cutting Themes for Content Creation

### 1. Anti-Content-Dump Philosophy
The entire series is built on the thesis that most online courses fail because they're designed as content delivery systems rather than learning experiences. This is Abe's strongest differentiating perspective — it draws directly from his HCI research on how people actually use information systems (vs. how designers assume they will).

### 2. Dual Minimalism as Design Philosophy
The MVO discussion crystallizes a principle that runs through all the sessions: **focused outcomes + minimal content**. This isn't about making courses short or cheap — it's about ruthless clarity on what matters. The 4 Scoping Questions are a practical tool for applying this principle. The "plane ride" question is especially powerful because it bypasses the expert's instinct to be comprehensive and forces focus on highest-impact content.

### 3. Backward Design (Understanding by Design)
The Know-Feel-Do framework and the emphasis on starting with outcomes before designing content is aligned with Wiggins & McTighe's "Understanding by Design" (backward design) methodology, which is the gold standard in instructional design. Abe makes it accessible to practitioners without using the academic jargon.

### 4. Cognitive Load as Design Constraint
"Inappropriate dosing" is Abe's practitioner-friendly term for cognitive overload. The balanced learning modules approach directly addresses Sweller's Cognitive Load Theory — vary activity types, control information density, alternate between input and application.

### 5. Social Learning Theory in Practice
Session 4's community framework is a practical application of Bandura's social learning theory and Wenger's communities of practice. The three approaches (social presence, discussion, live interaction) map to established constructs in online learning research.

### 6. The Expertise Reversal Effect
The "Curse of Knowledge" discussion is related to the expertise reversal effect (Kalyuga et al., 2003) — instructional methods that help novices can actually hinder experts, and vice versa. This is why expert course creators often design courses that don't work for beginners.

### 7. Iterative Design as Default
"You don't feel comfortable teaching the course until the 3rd time you run it" — this is design research methodology applied to course creation. Don't aim for perfection on the first try; aim for rapid iteration with real learners.
